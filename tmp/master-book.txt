The UAE Business Setup System in One ViewSource key: R1 MoET Free Zones | R2 UAE Government - Starting a Business in a Free Zone | R3 UAE Government - Mainland Steps | R4 Invest in Dubai - company set-up optionsThe UAE business setup market sits on three primary formation tracks: mainland, free zone, and offshore. Mainland companies are licenced by the relevant economic department or economic authority in the emirate and are generally the cleanest route for broad UAE onshore trade. Free-zone companies sit under a separate registrar and are usually chosen for foreign ownership, specialist ecosystems, export-focused operations, easier founder packages, or faster digital setup. Offshore structures are used mainly for holding, asset ownership, and cross-border structuring rather than day-to-day operating business inside the UAE.The Ministry of Economy and Tourism states that the UAE has more than 40 free zones. The ministry also publishes the registry map of the free-zone registrars by emirate, which makes it the best starting point for a national reference book. The federal government platform separately explains the free-zone setup flow: choose the legal form, choose the trade name, define the business activity, submit documents, obtain the licence, and then complete immigration steps if residency is required.The most common mistake founders make is choosing the cheapest package first and only later discovering that the chosen activity, market-access model, or banking profile is a poor fit. In practice, the right setup must match five things at the same time: activity approval, target market access, visa need, facility need, and tax/banking posture.TrackBest forMarket access logicVisa logicTypical cautionMainlandOnshore UAE trading, retail, local service delivery, domestic contractsCan generally operate inside and outside the UAE subject to activity rulesOwner / employee visas available through the mainland entityMay require extra approvals, sector conditions, or ownership restrictions for certain activitiesFree zoneExport, regional services, consulting, e- commerce, specialist clusters, low-friction founder entryExcellent for cross-border and B2B models; onshore trading usually needs additional licensing/distributi on structureRanges from 0- visa packages to multi-visa bundlesPackages are not apples-to-apples across zones; activity scope and facility rules vary widelyOffshoreHoldcos, IP ownership, family wealth, certain asset structures, international structuringUsually not designed for local operating tradeCommonly not a visa-first structureNot suitable as a substitute for a true operating company in the UAE2. Mainland vs Free Zone vs Offshore - How to ChooseSource key: R2 UAE Government - free zone setup | R4 Invest in Dubai - company set-up options | R5 Invest in Dubai - free-zone set-up costs | R6 Invest in Dubai - foreign ownership restrictionsChoose mainland when the business model depends on direct onshore UAE trade, local tenders, retail presence, or straightforward contracting inside the domestic market. Invest in Dubai states that mainland
companies can operate both within and outside the UAE, whereas free-zone companies need a specific route if they want to trade within the UAE market directly.Choose free zone when the business is export-led, digital-first, founder-led, regional, or ecosystem-driven. Free zones also work well for consulting, holding, many service businesses, many e-commerce structures, and early-stage businesses that want a registered UAE company without a full operating footprint on day one.Choose offshore when you need a non-operating structure, typically for ring-fencing ownership, holding shares, estate and succession planning, or certain international transactions. Offshore is not a shortcut around local licensing requirements for an operating business.Ą Do not start with price. Start with business model and activity.Ą If you need one legal entity to invoice UAE clients, hire staff, rent facilities, and sponsor families, test those needs first.Ą If banking credibility matters more than upfront setup cost, choose a structure with stronger substance and documentability.Ą If the real objective is a holding company, family office, or SPV, do not overpay for a trading-style zone package.Ą If the business may become regulated later, choose a jurisdiction that can support the next licence class, not only the first licence class.3. Legal Forms Commonly Seen in the UAESource key: R2 UAE Government - free zone setup | R3 UAE Government - mainland steps | R7 ADGM setting-up FAQs | R8 JAFZA company formation | R9 RAK ICCThe legal form is not just a filing choice. It affects ownership, governance, bank onboarding, audit obligations, and what the entity can grow into later. The federal and registrar guidance commonly points to the following operating forms:Ą Sole establishment / sole proprietorship - common for very small mainland activities, but the liability and banking profile should be considered carefully.Ą Limited Liability Company (LLC) - the default operating form for many mainland companies.Ą FZE / FZ-LLC / FZCO - common free-zone structures, typically single-shareholder or multi- shareholder limited liability vehicles depending on the registrar.Ą Branch - used by existing UAE or foreign companies that want a branch rather than a separately owned subsidiary.Ą Holding company - useful where the operating business should sit below a parent entity rather than be owned directly.Ą SPV - a special-purpose vehicle used for ring-fenced transactions or holdings rather than a broad operating trade.Ą Foundation / family office structure - relevant to wealth, succession planning, and governance rather than day-to-day trading.Ą Offshore company - a specialised cross-border vehicle used in selected jurisdictions such as JAFZA Offshore or RAK ICC products.4. Part II - Immigration, Family, Residency, and Banking6. Owner Visas, Employee Visas, and the Residency FlowSource key: R21 ICP residency permit service | R22 UAE Government - general residence provisions | R23 UAE Government - health conditions | R24 MOHRE work permits for family-sponsored dependentsMost founder-facing free-zone conversations oversimplify visas. In practice, the residency path has multiple layers: the company must exist and have the right immigration setup, the visa quota must exist or be expandable, the applicant must satisfy the document requirements, the applicant must undergo medical fitness procedures where applicable, and the residence permit issuance is linked to the Emirates ID application.ICP states that the residency permit issuance service is linked to the Emirates ID application. The UAE Government platform also states that residence applicants aged 18 and above must undergo a medical test and pass a security check. This means the visa stage is operationally inseparable from ID, medical, and compliance steps.For companies with staff beyond the founder, the labour/work-permit layer must also be mapped. If a dependent is already under family sponsorship, MOHRE provides a separate pathway for a work permit for family-sponsored dependents.Ą Map owner visa, employee visa, and dependent sponsorship separately. They are related, but not the same workflow.Ą Always check whether the package includes only visa eligibility or the full immigration cost stack.Ą Do not assume visa count is unlimited. Visa quota is often tied to facility type or package tier.Ą Medical, Emirates ID, and insurance can materially affect total cost even when the licence itself looks cheap.7. Family Sponsorship, Dependents, and Health InsuranceSource key: R25 UAE Government - family residence visa | R26 UAE Government - health insurance | R24 MOHRE family-sponsored dependent work permitFamily sponsorship is central to real setup planning because many founders are not only buying a company - they are buying relocation stability. The UAE Government states that a resident may sponsor family members if the sponsor meets the salary threshold, generally AED 4,000 or AED 3,000 plus accommodation. This is the federal baseline users normally need to know first.Health insurance is no longer a minor side note. The UAE Government states that from 1 January 2025 employers are required to purchase health insurance as a prerequisite for applying for the issuance or renewal of work residence permits. In operational terms, this means residency planning should now treat insurance as part of the visa budget, not an afterthought.Where dependents later want to work, MOHRE offers a work-permit path for family-sponsored dependents. This is strategically useful for founder families and for spouses who may not initially want to hold the main investor visa.Dependency layerWhy it mattersWhat the book should captureSpouse / child sponsorshipRelocation and family stabilityEligibility, cost, document stack, and timingDependent work permitFlexibility for spouses / adult dependentsMOHRE process, fees, and employer readinessHealth insuranceResidency issuance / renewal dependencyWhether employer-provided, package-provided, or separately procuredSchooling / housing proofsOften relevant in relocation realityPractical checklist rather than only legal checklist

A company may be incorporated successfully and still face onboarding friction if the structure, ownership chain, country profile, transaction profile, or document package is weak. Part III - Tax, E-Invoicing, UBO, and Compliance10. VAT - Registration, Filing, and Operating LogicSource key: R32 FTA VAT registration | R33 MoF VAT page | R34 FTA VAT filing and payment | R35 FTA VAT topicsVAT is one of the first compliance layers that a growing company hits. The FTA states that VAT registration is mandatory when a resident business exceeds AED 375,000 in taxable supplies and imports over the preceding 12 months or is expected to exceed that threshold in the next 30 days. The Ministry of Finance separately confirms a voluntary registration threshold of AED 187,500.Once registered, the FTA states that VAT returns and related payments must be filed within 28 days from the end of the tax period. The practical implication is simple: every founder should know whether the business is still below threshold, voluntarily registered for credibility/input VAT reasons, or already fully within mandatory-registration territory.The book should treat VAT as an operating system, not just a threshold. It affects invoicing, bookkeeping discipline, customer contracts, import flows, designated-zone treatment where relevant, and cash management. Every chapter for each zone should therefore include a VAT-readiness note, not just a generic tax paragraph.VAT questionCurrent federal answerWhen is VAT registration mandatory?When taxable supplies and imports exceed AED 375,000, or are expected to exceed that threshold within 30 daysWhen is voluntary registration possible?At or above AED 187,500 for taxable supplies/imports or qualifying expensesWhen do returns/payments fall due?Within 28 days from the end of the tax periodWhat should founders track early?Revenue trend, taxable vs exempt supplies, imports, invoicing method, and input VAT recovery potential11. Corporate Tax - Scope, Rates, QFZP, and FilingSource key: R36 MoF Corporate Tax in the UAE | R37 UAE Government - corporate tax | R38 MoF introduction of Corporate Tax | R39 FTA EmaraTax | R40 FTA filing deadline newsCorporate Tax applies nationally. The Ministry of Finance states that juridical persons established in a UAE free zone are also within the scope of Corporate Tax and must comply with the law. The idea that Ňfree zone means no Corporate Tax registrationÓ is therefore wrong. Registration still matters even when a favourable rate might apply to a portion of income.The Ministry of Finance and the UAE Government both state the current standard structure: 0% on taxable income up to AED 375,000 and 9% above that threshold for the standard regime, while a Qualifying Free Zone Person may benefit from 0% on Qualifying Income if the statutory conditions are met. This is why every serious free-zone chapter must separate the words Ňfree zoneÓ and ŇQFZPÓ rather than treating them as interchangeable.EmaraTax is the digital platform used to manage registration and filings. The FTA states that Corporate Tax returns and payments are generally due within nine months of the end of each tax period unless a specific decision extends the deadline for a defined cohort. For newly incorporated companies, the first tax period follows the first financial year or part of it for which a return is required.Ą Free-zone incorporation does not remove the need to register for Corporate Tax.Ą QFZP status should be analysed, not assumed.Ą Holding structures, family foundations, and SPVs need separate tax analysis rather than generic Ň0% taxÓ assumptions.
Ą Startups should set chart of accounts and bookkeeping discipline early enough to support the first tax period cleanly.12. Corporate Tax Penalties and Deadline DisciplineSource key: R41 FTA late payment / return penalty news | R42 FTA waiver page | R43 FTA late registration penalty waiver newsThe cost of late compliance in the UAE can quickly exceed the cost of doing the compliance properly. The FTA states that late submission of a Corporate Tax return or delay in settling Corporate Tax payable results in an administrative penalty of AED 500 per month, or part thereof, during the first twelve months, rising to AED 1,000 per month thereafter.For late Corporate Tax registration, the FTA introduced a waiver pathway. The FTA states that to benefit from the waiver of the penalty for late registration, the taxable person must submit the first tax return within seven months from the end of the first tax period, instead of the normal nine-month filing window, and complete the relevant registration/return steps through EmaraTax.This is one of the most important operating chapters in the book because many founders still think incorporation is the finish line. In reality, incorporation is the start of a recurring compliance calendar.13. E-Invoicing - What is Coming and Why It MattersSource key: R44 MoF eInvoicing portal | R45 MoF eInvoicing decisions newsThe UAE now has a published e-invoicing programme. The Ministry of Finance states that its eInvoicing portal is the official source of information on the programme. The ministry also announced the issuance of ministerial decisions on scope and implementation timelines in 2025.The Ministry of Finance states that the pilot programme will commence on 1 July 2026 with a selected taxpayer group. It also states that businesses with annual revenue of AED 50 million or more must appoint an accredited service provider by 31 July 2026 and implement the system from 1 January 2027 under the relevant ministerial timeline.For founders and SMEs, the immediate action is not to panic. It is to choose invoicing and accounting systems that can mature into structured e-invoicing later. The book should therefore treat e-invoicing as a system-design chapter, especially for anyone building finance tooling, ERP, or an all-in-one business setup platform.14. UBO, AML Readiness, and Economic SubstanceSource key: R46 MoET companies legislations | R47 MoET UBO workshop news | R48 MoET recent real beneficial owner article | R49 MoF ESR page | R50 MoF ESR amendment newsUltimate beneficial ownership is not an optional concept. The Ministry of Economy and Tourism publishes the current beneficial-owner legislation and related updates. In 2026 the ministry reiterated that the real beneficial owner is the natural person who ultimately owns or controls the legal person and holds, directly or indirectly, the right to appoint or dismiss the majority of directors or 25% or more of shares or voting rights. This is a core company-maintenance and KYC issue, not just a legal footnote.AML readiness matters most at onboarding and banking stages. Businesses that cannot clearly explain ownership, source of funds, business purpose, or transaction flow will struggle more with regulators, banks, and counterparties. This is especially important in cross-border trading, fintech, family-office structures, and high-risk jurisdictions.Economic Substance Regulations are now a historical-but-relevant topic. The Ministry of Finance states that ESR reporting requirements were cancelled for financial years ending after 31 December 2022. The book should still explain ESR because many older advisers, documents, and client assumptions continue to refer to it; however, the book should also make clear that the reporting requirement has been amended.

101. Visa and Immigration Workflow - Federal BaselineSource key: R90-R99Across the UAE, the immigration backbone is more standardised than many founders expect. The company-formation authority may differ, but the residence-permit sequence is broadly consistent.Ą Step 1 - Obtain the trade licence and company documents.Ą Step 2 - Activate the immigration file or establishment-card path required for the authority package.Ą Step 3 - Issue entry permit or complete in-country status adjustment where applicable.Ą Step 4 - Complete medical fitness for applicants aged 18 and above where residence rules require it.Ą Step 5 - Complete biometrics and Emirates ID application/processing.Ą Step 6 - Residence permit is issued; ICP states the residence permit service is linked to the Emirates ID application.Ą Step 7 - Secure health insurance where required for permit issuance, renewal, employer compliance, or family sponsorship.Ą Step 8 - Only after the owner/partner residence is active should dependants, staff visas, and most bank onboarding efforts be sequenced.The UAE government states that residence-visa applicants aged 18 and above must undergo medical testing and pass a security check, and the family sponsorship page confirms medical-fitness requirements for sponsored family members aged 18 and above. The Emirates ID page confirms the card is mandatory for UAE citizens and residents, while ICP states that residence permits are issued in connection with the Emirates ID application flow.102.
